Engrave + Contrast

A QR code sign is not just a decoration. It must scan on the first try. This scene should teach contrast, minimum size, quiet zone, material choice, and placement. TOOCAA can engrave crisp QR plates for restaurants, craft booths, retail displays, weddings, classrooms, and event check-ins when the design keeps readability first.

Quick answer

Use high contrast, keep the QR code at least 35-45 mm wide for close scanning, leave a clear quiet zone, and test the code with multiple phones before finishing.

QR Codes workflow

A practical sequence users can follow from idea to finished piece.

01

Generate the QR code

Use a short URL when possible and export the QR as SVG or high-resolution PNG.

02

Protect the quiet zone

Leave empty space around the QR code so the phone camera can read it cleanly.

03

Run a small scan test

Engrave a small test at the final size and scan it under warm, cool, and low light.

04

Add human text

Include a short label like Scan for menu or Scan for product specs so users know why to scan.

05

Finish without glare

Avoid glossy coatings over QR codes because reflections can reduce scan reliability.

Frequently asked questions

What size should a laser engraved QR code be?

For close-range signs, 35-45 mm is a practical minimum. Larger signs or wall-mounted QR codes should be much bigger.

Can a QR code be engraved on wood?

Yes, but contrast matters. Pale woods with dark engraving or dark-filled marks scan better than low-contrast hardwood engraving.

Why will my engraved QR code not scan?

Common reasons are low contrast, missing quiet zone, overly small size, smoke stains, glossy finish, or a broken source URL.

Should I cut or engrave the QR code?

Engrave the QR code. Cutting individual QR squares is usually too fragile and unnecessary for scanning.
