Laser cutting wood rewards small setup details. A flat board, clean focus, air assist, and a realistic first pass can be the difference between a crisp edge and a smoky, half-cut job. Use this guide to choose a wood, set up a wood laser cutter, compare plywood and MDF settings, and learn when to cut, engrave, or slow down for a cleaner result.

Quick answer

For a first project, use 3 mm basswood or birch plywood. Cut with air assist, 100% power, moderate speed, and one small test square before the final design.

Clean cut workflow

A repeatable process for better edges, fewer failed sheets, and more predictable laser cut wood projects.

01

Flatten and focus

Pin warped corners, set the laser focus at the top surface, and keep the workpiece level across the whole bed.

02

Mask the face

Apply low-tack masking tape or transfer tape before cutting pale wood. Peel after the job to remove smoke staining.

03

Turn on air assist

Air assist clears smoke from the kerf, cools the edge, reduces flame, and improves cut-through consistency.

04

Test before the design

Cut a small square with one inner circle. If the circle drops out cleanly, the larger job is much safer to run.

05

Finish lightly

Use a damp cloth, 320-grit sandpaper, or a soft brush to clean the edge. Avoid over-sanding engraved detail.

Safe to cut, cut with care, and avoid

Wood can burn quickly. Keep the machine supervised and treat smoke management as part of the setup, not an optional accessory.

Safe to laser cut

  • Basswood, balsa, poplar, maple
  • Birch and poplar plywood with known glue
  • Untreated solid hardwoods
  • Cork and clean cardboard

Cut with care

  • MDF with strong exhaust only
  • Pine or resinous wood
  • Painted or stained wood with unknown finish
  • Thick plywood with dense glue layers

Do not laser cut

  • PVC, vinyl, and faux leather with chlorine
  • Pressure-treated lumber
  • Fiberglass, carbon fiber, and epoxy boards
  • Unknown plastics laminated to wood

Before pressing start

  • Ventilation or fume extractor is running
  • Air assist is on and lens is clean
  • Fire extinguisher is reachable
  • The machine is supervised

Frequently asked questions

Short answers for the questions users search before buying or using a wood laser cutter.

What wood should I use first on a laser cutter?

Use 3 mm basswood or birch plywood. They cut faster than hardwood, show engraving clearly, and are forgiving while you learn focus, speed, and air assist.

Can a diode laser cut plywood?

Yes, but plywood quality matters. Thin birch or poplar plywood is best. Dense glue layers, knots, and internal gaps can stop the beam, so test each sheet before the final design.

How do I engrave wood without making it too dark?

Lower the power, raise the speed, and use a tighter line interval only when detail requires it. On dark woods like walnut, very low power often creates cleaner text than a heavy burn.

Why are the edges black after laser cutting wood?

Black edges usually come from too much heat, poor air flow, dirty optics, or resin in the board. Try air assist, masking, faster speed with an extra pass, and a cleaner low-resin wood.

Can I laser cut painted wood?

Only cut painted wood when you know the paint and substrate are laser-safe. Avoid unknown finishes, PVC-containing coatings, pressure-treated lumber, and any material with strong chemical odor.
